From Fundamentals of Software Architecture by Mark Richards and Neal Ford. Three short laws that frame how architects should think about every decision.

First Law: Everything in software architecture is a trade-off

No choice is a free win. Even code reuse introduces coupling and churn unless the reused code has good abstraction and low volatility — which is why plumbing code (frameworks, libraries, infrastructure) is usually a better reuse target than high-volatility domain logic.

Trade-off analysis also has to be repeated, even for situations that look similar: small contextual differences in teams, budgets, goals or technology can change which option is best. Understanding the trade-offs but weighting them wrong for the actual context is its own antipattern (Out of Context).

Second Law: Why is more important than how

Understanding the reasons and trade-offs behind a decision is more valuable than merely knowing the resulting structure. Future architects and developers need the rationale, not just the outcome — which is why architecture decision records (ADRs) and diagrams matter: they preserve the reasoning, context and consequences behind decisions, not just the resulting implementation.

Third Law: Most decisions exist on a spectrum, not as binaries

Reducing architectural choices to simple either-or answers is misleading; most involve gradations and contextual weighting. Classic examples:

  • A shared library can beat a shared service when callers are homogeneous and versioning is manageable — better performance, fault tolerance and change safety.
  • A shared service wins with heterogeneous platforms or high-volatility shared behavior, avoiding recompilation across many services.
  • Queues allow heterogeneous messages, independent monitoring and tighter control over consumers; topics reduce coupling and improve extensibility because new consumers can subscribe without changing the producer.

The takeaway

Architects should build a reputation as objective arbiters of trade-offs rather than evangelists for a favorite technology — context changes over time, and today's favored solution can become tomorrow's antipattern.
